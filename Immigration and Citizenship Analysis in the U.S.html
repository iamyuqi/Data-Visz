Topic Introduction

As a typical immigrant country, the U.S. welcomes a huge number of immigrants from all over the world each year. The government provides several approaches, including working, marriage, humanitarian, etc. to allow foreigners to accomplish their American dream and attain lawful permanent resident (LPR) status. There are lots of immigrant data worth to explore and visualized. Furthermore, it can be helpful to international students who wants to know more about this country and its immigrant data. By doing dataset research and data visualization, the immigrant situation and policy of this country can be better understand.

Data Source

a. USCIS

The main data source are from www.uscis.gov and www.migrationpolicy.org.

U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services is the government agency oversees lawful immigration to the United States. [1] They provide summarized immigration and citizenship data on their website. The dataset are divided into eight parts:

  • Deferred action for childhood arrivals(DACA)
  • Family based
  • Employment based
  • Humanitarian
  • Citizenship and naturalization
  • Frequently requested statistics
  • Forms
  • Archives
    • In this visualization project, employment and performance data were applied to see H1-b trends, enterprises distributions and case volume and pending duration.

    b. MPI


    The Migration Policy Institute is an independent research institute who provides analysis, development, and evaluation of migration and refugee policies at local, national, and international levels. [2] The statistic data is from MPI Data Hub: Acquisition of U.S. Citizenship by Country of Birth (Fiscal Year 1999-2015) and Foreign-Born Population by State: 1990, 2000, 2010, and 2015. The data was used to see the composition of immigrant population and trend of foreign population's residential state.

    Data Products

    a. Tableau Visualization for research question 1 & 2.

    The left slopgraph indicates how original citizenship of immigrant poplation change during the years and the right heatmap shows which states immigrants would like to resident. Above the map, The foreign population in 1990 and 2015 was plotted to visually compare each state's population change between two years.

    Observations

    1. By adjusting year range in the filter, it is easily to see how immigrant population change during the years. The first chart shows a population decrease of Americas and Asia and an increase of Africa from 1999 to 2007. While from then until 2015, the population of Americas and Asia increase. But overall, there is a decrease trend for those two countries. The population rank can be obviously seen from the chart: Americas, Asias, Europe Africa, etc. and the rank never change during the years.
    2. The most popular state is California in 2005, followed by Texas, New York and Florida. There is no apparently geographical factors accoring to the map. The popular states all have better education resources and employment opportunities and that might be the reason with greater attraction compared to other states.
    3. From the plot, California always had the highest foreign born population and there was small change between 1990 and 2015. Texas had the largest change between two years. Most of the states had an increase of foreign born population. States themself had very few foreign population like Montana, South/North Dokota, Vermont, West Virginia and Wyoming, demonstrate very little change.

    Observations

    • SWOT Analysis

      Strength

    • Data source is from official cite, so the accuracy can be reliable.
    • Adequate data and clear format make data cleaning process easier.
    • Data products can help to intuitive understand the immigrant fact in recent years.

      • Weakness

    • There are already many related visualizations, since the data is public.
    • Hard to make connection between immigrant data and economic data.
    • Most of data products are observations (canâ€™t make predictions or suggestions since immigration policy will change anytime).

      • Opportunity

    • There are plenty of data from online sources (surveys, economic census) can help to find correlation with immigrant data.
    • Date output might benefit international students whether applying h1b visa.

      • Thread

    • Confidential data will limit the findings on data product.
    • There is no significant change or trend appearing in visualizations.
